History of farming in India: Where it all started

To know the farmer bills, we must know the history of farming sales and the different stages of food production and distribution. In the beginning, right after the independance act of 1947, farmers directly sold to the consumers. But this system caused the farmers to go into debt, due to other payments needed to be made such as rent and others. This made the farmers have to go to money-lenders who bought their produce at a substantially low price. To balance their loss, farmers had to take huge loans that they weren't able to pay back. To help the farmers, the government brought the APMC act, or the Agricultral Produce Market Commitee act. The APMC act stated that no farmer can directly sell produce to a consumer, and that all sales must take place in a mandi ([farmer] market). Mandis are located in divided up areas across states, and are mandotory by law. For a trader to buy from a mandi, they need to have a license. The produce is then auctioned off in two different ways, the first using the MSP or minimun support price. This is a fixed rate that is used as the minimum bid for the auction. The other system is price discovery, this is simply the price based on supply and demand. After the farmer has sold their product, it passes through a chain of middle-men until it reaches the consumer. Ultimately the farmer gets only about 15% of what the consumer bought it for.



The APMC act was first made to help the farmers from losing money, but rather it made it worse as the years passed. This is because the APMC act introduced MSP, and though it restricted buying from farmers at ridiculously low prices, it gave an oppurtunity to traders to force farmers to sell to them at the lowest price the MSP allowed them to go. Since farmers didn't have a choice on selling their products, they had to sell it at a very low price that couldn't feed them or their families, forcing them to borrow money again falling into an almost unleaveable cycle.

What are the Farmer Bills?

Now that you know a brief history of the farmer laws in India and how their sales work, we can understand how the new bills imposed by the Modi government are anti-farmer. The main idea of the laws are to remove the MSP and mandis, removing the mandis means no market for the farmer to auction their produce, meaning farmers will have to sell it to whoever comes to them. The removal of the MSP means that there isn't a starting price for selling and the produce can be sold a very low costs. Removing both of these things together will eventually lead to only a few big buyers, corporates. This is because, since the mandis are removed, farmers can't go to the previous customers of the auction, traders (middlemen) and even the government. This leaves only big corporates to sell to, who have enough storage for the produce. These big tycoons like Gautam Adani and Mukesh Ambani, the richest businessmen of India, will buy from farmers at extremely low prices. This can be backed up by another law the government of India passed which states that farmers cannot legally sue corporates. This clearly shows that the government knows the outcome of their laws is a big profit for corporates.



One buyer left

The new bills removing mandis and the MSP, allow only one buyer for farmers, corporates or businessmen. This can be confirmed by another law the government passed stating farmers can't take legal action against corporates specifically.

No mandis, no life

Mandis, farmer markets, where farmers used to sell their produce on auction are being removed, said to give freedom to farmers and allow them to sell wherever they want. In reality this removal gives corporates easy access to cheap produce.

Cheap Produce

In addition to removing mandis, the Indian government has removed the MSP (Minimum Support Price). Meaning farmers' produce can be bought for extremely low prices.

The degradation of the APMC Act

The APMC act which was first made to help farmers escape middlemen, soon didn't fit in with the evolving system and made farmers more prone to middlemen who took most of the money let alone allow the farmer to make any profit. But while the new bills remove these issues, they make it much worse for farmers, since they have to worry about selling their produce at an extremely low price, that they will barely be able to survive.
